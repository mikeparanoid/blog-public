Tripping a Foot-gun I Set For Myself: Keepass.

on 2022-10-04

Background

I have been using Keepass for 6 years minimum now. When I first started using it was around the time I was learning how to program, and thus when I was learning how to use computers. This meant that I was foolish and naive and thought I new everything (I doubt that much has changed) . So, in my awesome wisdom, I engineered a system with Keepass that was not fail-safe, and a system that was a massive foot-gun for my future self, which I recently shot myself with.

So at the time, I had set Keepass up with both a keyfile and a master password. I put the keyfile onto every device that I would use to access the database (work computer, university account, home computer, phone) and I then I would carry a USB that had the Keepass database on it. I would synch this USB onto a specific USB on my home PC, and I also would have an encrypted backup copy of this Database on my local PC. It seemed totaly logical to me then, to have only a single instance of the database, and spread multiple keys on whatever devices I would need.

Long story short, I lost the Keepass USB I had at my Home PC when I moved house, then my PC hard drive failed (maybe something to do with the move?), and then I lost my wallet a few days later. Within the space of a week I lost all my passwords and the backups! What a bloody disaster! You can imagine the horror that I went through. After unpacking, the USB did not turn up, I called every cop-shop to see if my wallet turned up. Nope, it had not.

Recovery

Luckily, I could reset the password for my main Google account via text message, which then I slowly could reset passwords to all the services that I use daily that I am happy to be identified with: bank, phone, Amazon, eBay, etc. Also, my "main" junk emails that I use for social media, Steam, etc. are recoverable through this main email account. Slowly, I was able to work my way through my main email and recover about 25% of lost accounts. The most difficult was recovering my Github account because I had no access to the registered email account, but I still had access to SSH keys on my laptop that I used to verify my account, and reset the passwords (good on Microsoft for having decent support on the Github team).

Trying to keep a positive outlook: I got a free spring-cleaning of my password database. I have been using single-use email address for many random services and I did not even remember what half of them were used for, so losing access to these services was no big deal.

What I have learned.

What I learned is to no longer assume things, and that I should engineer even against the improbable. Million-to-one odds happen every 9 in 10 hey.

So for the next iteration of my Keepass password, I have decided to go the opposite route: I store the password database in a single location (currently on Google Drive) that I keep synced on all devices that I want to have access on. I might even see if I can host this on a website or Cloud device, so that only ever "see" a single version of the database, and thus not have to think about keeping any database synchronised.

Then, on my USB drives, I keep the key file. I now still have a USB on my computer desk as a backup, but I also have written down the key file and have this piece of paper filed away as a last-resort backup.

Thus, the only even that I cause me total loss if I somehow loose all the USBs, and key-paper at once (which has made me realise that I vulnerable to house fire, so I now also store a copy of the key at my work office). Or if Google decide to kick me off their platform, which is actually a concern. Maybe it is time to look at a viable alternative to Google for email and cloud storage ... great idea for a quick project!
